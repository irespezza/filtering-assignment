Cause of Death by Borough

By Irene Spezzamonte, Aliyah Veal, and Virginia Jeffries
Although Terry Guarino followed a healthy lifestyle, inclinding long-distance running and a healthy diet, the extensive hours behind the wheel may have taken a toll on her heart.

At the young age of 56, just 24 hours after a 5-mile run, Guarino was in an ambulance suffering cardiac arrest.

"I was in bed chatting with my husband and I started feeling like something was off," said Guarino, now 58.

Heart disease is the leading cause of death in the U.S. and in all five New York City boroughs, according to the latest data collected by the New York State Department of Health.

Where you live specifically might have an impact on whether you survive, according to David Satcher M.D., former surgeon general under Presidents Bill Clinton and George W. Bush, and pioneer in the field of social medicine.

"Where you live has a huge impact on your lifespan," said Dr. Satcher. "It determines what nutrition is available to you and even if where you live is a safe place to be physically active."

Researchers say that many health disparities are embedded within each borough and even within the different neighborhoods. That is why it is important to look at patients’ locations to determine their health risks.

“We all tend to be more responsive to the environment around us than we would like to think,” said Dr. Andrew Goodman, physician, professor at NYU, who has worked for the City Health Department for over 30 years.

“If we’re in a neighborhood with heavy duty promotion of sugary drinks and it’s readily available people will tend to consume more of those than if you’re in an environment that has fewer than that.”

Almost two years after her cardiac arrest, Guarino started running again, although she has a defibrillator implant inside her heart.

“I am back physically but not psychologically,” she said. “I hope this doesn’t happen again.”
